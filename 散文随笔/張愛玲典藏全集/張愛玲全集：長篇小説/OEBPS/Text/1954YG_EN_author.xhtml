About Eileen Chang

Eileen Chang (Chang Ai-Ling) was an immensely popular novelist and short story writer and a giant of Chinese literature. Born in Shanghai in 1920, she was an individualist in an insistently conformist society, and a cosmopolitan artist in a provincial literary landscape. She came to the United States in 1955 and died a recluse and a cult figure in Los Angeles in 1995. Her New York Times obituary quoted experts who compared her favorably with such widely acclaimed writers as Eudora Welty, Flannery O’Connor, and Franz Kafka, and said she would have won a Nobel Prize had it not been for the political division between the Nationalist and Communist Chinese.

Despite her great fame among Chinese-language readers around the world, her work has been nearly unavailable in English in this country for many years. With the reissue of The Rice-Sprout Song, this important writer is introduced to a new generation of readers who will appreciate Chang ’s elegant and restrained prose style, her harrowing analyses of human motives, and her keen understanding of desire, loneliness, and hunger both physical and metaphysical.
